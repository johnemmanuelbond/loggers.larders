### Timeline

#### Days 1-3

Your trip will start out on the southern spur of the Wyfloe Delta, pushing off from Map Camp with the cheery wayfinder Alberta de Genne. Compared to the festival weather from about a week prior the season has begun to turn, there's a breeze whistling through the brown and drying reeds under the late summer sun and the nights are getting cold. The river turns westward and your first few days are spent dodging around floating rafts of mud and peat matted with grasses and vegetation. You spend your nights looking for some, any, solid ground to camp on as both sides of the river are surrounded by thick marshes.

Over the days Alberta starts pointing out other branches of the delta, splitting off and headed north towards what you presume is just more of the same landscape. After each of these distributaries the river widens, and widens some more until the true scale of this river outlet comes into view. Perpetually, in the distance, you can make out the heights of the snow-peaked mountains to the south and gather that all of that snow has to melt, eventually, and funnel down through the rocks out through this very point. It's that force you're paddling against, and you're grateful it's not as strong as it might be after winter.
  
#### Days 4-6

On the third day you begin to make out a change in the color of the grasses. The tall reeds recede into green meadows before coming to the sharp line of a deep forest. It's on this fourth day where you pass the final branch of the Wyfloe delta, larger than all you've seen before combined, and the river reaches it's full 2-mile width. On your left (south) the interspersed marshes and grasslands continue to line the foothills in the shadows of the mighty Mountain of Melian. On your right (north) the river banks are lined with enormous old-growth conifers and thick oaks with gnarled roots reaching into the river between sections of ferns and leaf-litter.

Occasionally you can make out a forest squirrel with an acorn in its mouth, anxiously gathering food to stow for winter, which grows closer with every week. On the fifth day you see a herd of familiar deer lapping water from the river. One has a pair of impressive, white, saber-like tusks emerging from his snout as he looks out over the river for threats. Alberta is quick to point out each of these sightings to describe how these, wild, Saber-Deer are different from their domesticated cousins on the Aire steppe.

#### Days 7-9

On the seventh day you notice the first tributary of the Wyfloe coming in rapids from the hills to the south. The paddling is particularly hard, but with Alberta's help and experience you get through them. She explains how her past trip explored up that river only to come to an impassible cataract. She would have had to carry a canoe up a sheer rock face to get past it!

The river bends a little southwest before the second tributary comes from the north; breaking up the otherwise monotonous forest edge. Alberta directs you around a great brown bear at the point where this other stream joins in. It pays you no mind as it uses it's huge paws to fish for salmon.

The third tributary comes from the south again. Here, at the start of the eighth day, the river finally begins to narrow as Alberta takes you down the northerly of two seemingly equal branches. She says she just happened to choose north when she was here last, and while she knows there's something interesting up this branch, she can tell by its pace the other branch should be navigable in it's own right. Maybe, if this expedition goes smoothely (she chuckles), you can accompany her that way before winter forces you back to Harport.

#### Day 10

At the end of the ninth day you pull your canoes ashore and camp at the edge of the river. Alberta gives you a low-down on what to expect:

"So, friends, I cannot lie this is very exciting. I'm going to lead you to this clearing and by the way its arranged there's no way it can be natural. The meadow itself is almost perfectly circular, about 200 feet across, and this weird rock formation just stops right at the middle! On top of that, I think there's something living in it! I'm pretty quiet myself and got close enough to see an, I don't know, masoned(?) texture on the inside of its cave. If this is what I think, its a remnant of something truly ancient, so we need to be careful not to disturb anything."

The trek to the site takes about 6 hours of cutting through thick taiga underbrush, and you reach the clearing Alberta spoke of, dominated by this strange mound, and the rush of curiosity.
